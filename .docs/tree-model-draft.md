# The Tree Model: A Botanical Reading of SAE × EPIAS

*Draft for John Maeda — Noah Ratzan, March 2026*

---

## The Idea

Your tree-shaped designer diagram got me thinking about what the tree metaphor actually implies if you take it seriously. In botany, the shape of a tree tells you everything about its strategy. A Redwood and a fig tree are both successful — but they solve completely different problems. The same is true for designers in the AI era.

Here's the key move: **L0 is the ground.** Everything below ground is your root system — your craft foundation. Everything above ground is your AI-augmented growth. EPIAS describes both: below ground, it's how deep and extensive your roots are. Above ground, it's how tall you grow — your leadership reach.

## L0 as Ground Level

The EPIAS stages at L0 form concentric root zones:

- **Explorer** roots are shallow — you're learning craft basics, just getting established
- **Practitioner** roots go deeper — consistent manual workflow, reliable quality
- **Integrator** roots spread wider — design embedded end-to-end, decisions documented
- **Architect** roots form a network — reusable templates others use, structural support
- **Steward** roots are the deepest and most extensive — setting craft standards, mentoring, sustaining the whole ecosystem

A designer with deep L0 roots can support enormous growth above ground. A designer with shallow roots will topple in a strong wind, no matter how tall they've grown.

## Above Ground: Height × Spread

Above the ground line, two things happen at once:

- **Outward spread** is your SAE level. L1 stays close to the trunk. L2 reaches further. L3 and L4 extend wide. L5 is the broadest canopy.
- **Upward growth** is your EPIAS leadership stage. Explorer is just above the soil. Steward is the crown.

The combination of spread and height determines what kind of tree you are.

## A Taxonomy of Designer Trees

| Position | Tree | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **L1 Explorer** | Grass | Everywhere, low to the ground. The most common form on earth. |
| **L1 Steward** | Birch | Elegant, moderate height. Distinctive presence but not massive. Deep enough roots for its size. |
| **L2 Steward** | Maple | Strong canopy, reliable shade. The backbone of most design teams. |
| **L3 Steward** | Oak | Wide, deep-rooted, long-lived. Anchors the forest. |
| **L5 Explorer** | Fig / Banyan | Spreads endlessly outward — enormous horizontal reach. But without leadership height, it stays broad and low, growing nonstop without clear structure. |
| **L5 Steward** | Redwood | Enormously wide AND tall. Requires the deepest root system of all. The rarest form. |
| **L0 Steward** | Bluestem / American Beach Grass | The deepest, most extensive root system — but stays at ground level. Holds the soil together. Sustains the ecosystem that lets the trees grow. Not a Redwood, but the Redwood can't exist without it. |

The beauty of this: **every tree form is valid.** A meadow of grass is not a failed forest. An Oak is not an incomplete Redwood. The framework doesn't rank these — it describes them.

## What This Means for the Assessment

Right now the assessment places you on the matrix and points you "up and to the right." But the tree model suggests something different:

1. **Your L0 depth matters as much as your AI level.** The assessment should reflect and validate the strength of your roots, not just how far your branches reach.

2. **Reinforcing L0 is a good idea.** Some designers who score high on AI adoption may have shallow roots. The tree model says: go deep before you go wide.

3. **Two scores, not one.** A designer's profile is really two things: their root depth (L0 × EPIAS) and their canopy shape (SAE level × EPIAS above ground). The assessment could show both — your grounding and your growth.
